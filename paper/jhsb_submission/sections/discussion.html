Discussion

Summary of Findings

Our findings reveal that partisan differences in health behaviors during the COVID-19 pandemic were not only substantial but often exceeded differences between other demographic groups. The Democrat-Republican gap in mask usage, for instance, was larger than differences by gender and, perhaps more surprisingly, larger than age-based differences, despite the elderly facing dramatically higher mortality risk. These partisan gaps cannot be dismissed as artifacts of where Democrats and Republicans happen to live: they persisted within the same policy environments, across different geographic regions, and regardless of local partisan context. Nor can they be explained by demographic composition; the gaps remained after adjusting for age, education, and other covariates. Republicans reported more daily contacts, wore masks less frequently, and expressed lower vaccine acceptance than Democrats across all survey waves, and these differences proved remarkably robust to every alternative explanation we tested.

Yet while partisan gaps proved robust to demographics, geography, and policy, they were not static over time. The magnitude of polarization shifted across different stages of the pandemic, responding to changing conditions and perceptions of severity. This temporal variation is theoretically revealing: if partisan differences were purely a matter of fixed group characteristics or stable ideological commitments, we would expect consistent gaps regardless of context. Instead, we find that pandemic severity, particularly local mortality, shaped the degree of partisan divergence, and it did so differently for different behaviors. Our regression analyses show that mortality rates were the strongest predictor of contact behavior for all partisan groups; incidence rates, by contrast, had no significant effect. This distinction matters: case counts are abstract statistics, easily dismissed or reinterpreted, while deaths are concrete and visible, reported in local news, experienced through social networks, marked by obituaries and funerals. Partisan gaps in social contacts narrowed during high-mortality periods, particularly during the winter 2020-21 surge, suggesting that this kind of undeniable, proximate consequence can temporarily override partisan divergence. Mask-wearing, by contrast, showed increasing polarization over time, with the Democrat-Republican gap nearly tripling from 5 percentage points in September 2020 to 15 percentage points by May 2021. Unlike contacts, mask-wearing differences did not narrow when deaths surged.

What might explain these divergent patterns? The narrowing of contact gaps during high-mortality periods appears driven primarily by changes among those most concerned about COVID-19. When deaths surged, the threat became concrete and immediate, and respondents reporting high concern reduced their contacts sharply. Because Democrats were more likely to report high concern, this concern-driven response disproportionately affected Democratic behavior, pulling it closer to Republican levels during surges. The gap narrowed not because Republicans increased precautions, but because the most concerned (disproportionately Democrats) responded most strongly to rising mortality.

Wave 1 stands out as particularly distinctive. In April 2020, partisan gaps in both contacts and concern were at their smallest. This early convergence likely reflects multiple factors: the novelty and uncertainty of the initial outbreak, when the virus was new and partisan scripts had not yet solidified; but also the fact that the virus was especially deadly in these early months. Case fatality rates were at their highest before treatments improved and clinical protocols developed. The combination of novelty, uncertainty, and high lethality may have produced the conditions under which partisans responded similarly. As mortality rates declined and the pandemic became increasingly politicized, these gaps widened. The early pandemic also saw public health authorities shift their guidance on masks, initially advising against them before reversing course [CITE]. This reversal, combined with declining trust in public health institutions among both Democrats and Republicans over the course of the pandemic [CITE], may have contributed to the growing polarization. Yet shared erosion of trust cannot fully explain the partisan divergence: Republicans lost trust at substantially higher rates than Democrats [CITE]. Importantly, this differential did not originate with COVID-19. Scholars have documented a growing partisan gap in trust toward scientific and public health institutions dating back to at least 2009 [CITE], a divide already evident in differential vaccine uptake during the H1N1 outbreak [CITE]. The pandemic, then, did not create partisan differences in trust; it activated and amplified a fissure that had been widening for over a decade.

Masks followed a different trajectory entirely. Unlike contact reduction, mask-wearing showed no narrowing during mortality surges. In fact, during the winter 2020-21 surge, when contact gaps reached their narrowest point, mask-wearing gaps were simultaneously approaching their peak. Partisan polarization, it seems, is not a fixed quantity that rises and falls uniformly, but a persistent force that finds expression where it can. This divergence may reflect the different nature of these behaviors. Reducing contacts is a relatively private decision with high personal costs: fewer contacts means fewer social interactions, less economic activity, and greater isolation. The costs of not reducing contacts during a surge are also personal and immediate. Mask-wearing, by contrast, is visible, low-cost, and had become deeply politicized by the time of the winter surge. The conditions that drove contact convergence simply did not apply.

Perhaps the most striking finding is the disconnect between stated concern and actual behavior. During the winter 2020-21 surge, Republicans reported their lowest levels of COVID-19 concern across all waves. Yet, this was when they reduced their contacts most substantially and where behavior converged the most. That is, expressed concern may have become partly performative: Republicans responding as they believed a Republican should respond, signaling appropriate group sentiment, while their behavior aligned with the objective risk environment. Notably, our survey never asked respondents about their partisan identity; this information was collected by the survey panel at a time before our survey was administered. The disconnect between what Republicans said and what they did was not an artifact of priming or survey context. This pattern is consistent with what we would expect if partisanship operates as a social identity: attitudes serve as markers of group loyalty, signaling allegiance to one’s political tribe, while behavior responds to material consequences that affect personal well-being.

Why, then, might masks have served as lower-stakes ground for partisan differentiation? Compared to contact reduction, wearing a mask is a low-cost action—it requires no fundamental change to one’s social or economic life. But masks are also visible. Unlike the private decision of whether to attend a gathering, mask-wearing occurs in public spaces where others can observe it. This visibility transforms masks from a purely protective behavior into a potential signal of group membership. Wearing a mask—or conspicuously not wearing one—communicates something to others about who you are and what you believe, but mostly which group you belong to.

The scientific uncertainty surrounding mask efficacy may have amplified this dynamic. While the epidemiological evidence for reducing contacts was relatively straightforward—fewer interactions mean fewer transmission opportunities—the evidence on mask effectiveness was more contested, with guidance changing over the course of the pandemic and ongoing debates about mask types, fit, and real-world effectiveness [CITE]. This ambiguity created space for motivated reasoning: partisans could selectively attend to evidence that supported their group’s position. When the science is clear and consequences are immediate, there is less room for partisan interpretation; when the science is contested and consequences are diffuse, identity can more easily shape both belief and behavior.

The result is that mask-wearing may have become a form of strategic interaction, a performance of partisan identity for an observing audience [CITE Goffman 1959]. If the goal is maintaining unity and standing within one’s political tribe, then these performances are entirely rational, even if they elevate personal health risk. For Democrats, wearing a mask performed adherence to public health guidance, trust in scientific institutions, and solidarity with a collective response to the pandemic [CITE Dems trust science]. For Republicans, not wearing a mask could perform skepticism of government overreach, prioritization of personal freedom, and alignment with political leaders who downplayed the pandemic’s severity [CITE Reps skepticism/freedom framing]. Crucially, these identity games were enabled by the relatively low stakes of mask-wearing: visible enough to signal tribal allegiance, yet low-cost enough that the personal consequences of “playing” remained modest. Contact behavior offered no such opportunity. Reducing contacts is neither visible to one’s coalition nor easily performed; it happens privately. And the stakes are higher, the consequences more concrete. This asymmetry could explain why mask usage became increasingly partisan over time regardless of changes in severity, while contact gaps responded to mortality: masks were terrain on which partisan games could be played; contacts were not.

The persistence of partisan gaps across diverse settings provides further evidence that identity, rather than context, drives these behaviors. If local environment were the primary determinant, we would expect Republicans in high-risk areas to dramatically reduce the gap between them and their Democratic neighbors; we would expect identical policy environments, such as mask mandates or stay-at-home orders, to eliminate behavioral differences; and we would expect distinct regional cultures, such as those in the South, to override partisan distinctions. Yet, partisan gaps persisted within high-mortality counties, within states with stringent public health measures, and across geographic regions with vastly different political cultures. This “portable polarization” suggests that partisan identity travels with individuals, shaping behavior regardless of where they live, what policies govern them, or what risks surround them. The gaps are not artifacts of Republicans and Democrats sorting into different environments; they are features of partisan identity itself.

Yet local context is not irrelevant. While partisan gaps persisted across settings, their magnitude varied in ways that suggest local environment still exerts some influence. In the South, for example, partisan polarization in health behaviors was smallest, not because Republicans became more cautious, but because Democrats in these regions behaved less cautiously, reaching toward Republican levels. This pattern raises a question: do partisans adjust their behavior to match co-partisans in their local context? The evidence suggests they do, and again, mask-wearing proves most susceptible to this adjustment. Democrats in Republican-leaning counties wore masks less frequently than Democrats elsewhere; Republicans in Democratic-leaning counties wore masks more frequently than Republicans elsewhere. Contact behavior showed weaker contextual effects. This pattern is consistent with our framework of strategic interaction: if belonging to one’s local community carries value beyond national partisan allegiance, and if mask-wearing is the visible, low-stakes terrain on which group membership is performed, then we would expect masks to be the domain where local context most shapes behavior. Partisans appear to balance two forms of belonging, adjusting their visible performances to fit both their partisan tribe and their geographic community.

Yet the story is not so clean. While Democrats become less cautious in Republican regions, so do Republicans. Local context does not simply shrink the partisan gap; it shifts the overall level of cautiousness for both groups. In Republican-leaning areas, Republicans may feel an even stronger pull to signal their partisan identity through reduced mask usage, while Democrats adjust toward local norms but maintain their relative position. The result is that the partisan gap persists even as the baseline shifts. This reinforces rather than undermines our central argument: partisan identity remains a crucial determinant of behavior even within local contexts that push everyone in the same direction. Local belonging and partisan belonging operate simultaneously, with local context setting the level and partisan identity preserving the difference.

These local context effects, however, are difficult to interpret causally. Two explanations are plausible. The first is selection: Democrats who happen to be more similar to Republicans in their attitudes and behaviors may move to, or choose to remain in, Republican-leaning areas for reasons that have little to do with partisan influence. The second is absorption: partisans living among opposite-party majorities may gradually absorb the values and behaviors of their local peers over time. Our cross-sectional data cannot distinguish between these mechanisms. Future research should address this question with panel data that follows the same individuals over time, allowing researchers to observe whether attitudes and behaviors shift as local contexts change, rather than relying on comparisons across individuals in different settings as we do here.

Implications for Public Health

Our findings show that partisan identity is a strong predictor not only of health behavior but also of stated concern about the pandemic. This pattern suggests that information asymmetry may have been at play. Through mechanisms of mass media, where partisan news ecosystems deliver divergent messages about risk, or through network effects, where information flows more readily within than across partisan communities, Republicans may have received systematically different signals about pandemic severity than Democrats [CITE]. It is also possible that Republican-leaning regions are more geographically or socially clustered and isolated from other regions, creating structural barriers that prevent health information from disseminating easily into these communities.

These possibilities point to a broader research agenda. Understanding partisan networks along dimensions of partisan homophily, the tendency for partisans to associate with co-partisans, remains underdeveloped [CITE if available]. Equally important are the structural features of these networks: How clustered are partisan communities within themselves? How interconnected are they with ideologically dissimilar regions? Answering these questions has implications not only for designing more effective communication strategies to disseminate disease risk information across partisan divides, but also for improving disease transmission models. If partisan networks shape both information flow and physical contact patterns, then understanding their structure becomes essential for predicting how future outbreaks might spread through a polarized population.

These findings imply that effective public health policy aimed at reducing risk and encouraging protective behaviors should perhaps take partisan identity into account. Careful attention to how partisans differentially receive and respond to public health messages may be necessary for reaching the populations most resistant to adopting recommended behaviors. Previous research has explored tailoring health messages to specific demographic groups, such as by age, race, or education level [CITE]. Given our increasingly polarized society, it may be time to consider tailoring messages based on partisan identification as well, designing communications that resonate with the values, trusted messengers, and informational ecosystems of different partisan communities.

Our findings also suggest that perceived severity is a key driver of behavioral polarization. When Republicans perceived the threat as serious, as in the early weeks of the pandemic when lethality was highest, partisan gaps were smallest. Understanding the determinants of when Republicans consider a health threat more or less serious could translate into more effective public health messaging. If communicators can identify frames, messengers, or types of evidence that increase perceived severity among Republicans without triggering partisan resistance, they may be able to narrow behavioral gaps. The challenge, of course, is that efforts to increase adherence among one group must not inadvertently decrease adherence among the other.

This tension points to a broader dilemma. By acknowledging partisan identity in public health messaging, we may make interventions more effective while simultaneously reifying the importance of partisanship in American society. Tailoring messages to Republicans and Democrats treats them as distinct groups with fundamentally different orientations toward health and science, potentially further stratifying society along partisan lines. Public health experts will need to decide: do they treat polarization itself as a public health problem to be addressed, working to bridge divides and rebuild shared trust in scientific institutions? Or do they acknowledge polarization as a fact of American life and operate within it, accepting partisan divisions as given in order to reduce the loss of life they produce? A potential middle path may exist: crafting messages that appeal to shared values and concerns, finding common ground that resonates with both Republicans and Democrats without alienating either. Such an approach would require identifying frames that transcend partisan identity rather than targeting it, emphasizing aspects of health threats that both groups find compelling. This is not merely a strategic question but an ethical one, with implications that extend beyond any single health crisis.

Any such effort must also consider the partisan composition of the scientific and public health fields themselves. Scientists are disproportionately likely to identify as Democrats, and public health professionals are even more so [CITE]. This homogeneity may contribute to the perception of science and public health as partisan institutions, a perception that either validates trust among Democrats or undermines it among Republicans. Part of the solution may involve actively seeking partisan diversity both among those who produce scientific knowledge and among those who disseminate it. A more diverse field may be less likely to be perceived as a partisan institution, and more likely to generate messages that resonate across partisan lines.

Limitations

Several limitations should be considered when interpreting our findings.

First, our data are observational and cross-sectional, which limits our ability to make causal claims. We observe associations between partisan identity and health behaviors, but we cannot definitively establish that partisanship causes these behavioral differences rather than correlating with unmeasured confounders. Although we control for a range of demographic and contextual factors, residual confounding remains possible.

Second, our survey waves capture only a portion of the pandemic, from April 2020 to May 2021. We do not observe behaviors before the pandemic began, which would provide a baseline for understanding how partisans differed in their pre-pandemic contact patterns and health behaviors. Nor do we observe the later stages of the pandemic, including the Delta and Omicron waves, when vaccination became more widespread and pandemic fatigue may have altered behavioral patterns. Our findings speak to a specific period of the pandemic and may not generalize to its full trajectory.

Third, all behavioral measures are self-reported. Respondents may have over- or under-reported their contact counts, mask usage, or vaccination intentions due to social desirability bias or recall error. If such biases differed systematically by partisanship, for example, if Republicans were less likely to over-report mask usage, this could affect our estimates of partisan gaps. However, the consistency of our findings across multiple behavioral measures and contextual comparisons provides some reassurance that the patterns we observe are not artifacts of reporting bias alone.

Fourth, while we examine variation across multiple contextual dimensions, we cannot observe actual disease transmission. We measure behaviors that are relevant to transmission risk, but we cannot directly link these behaviors to infection outcomes. The relationship between the behavioral patterns we document and the partisan mortality disparities that motivate this study remains inferential rather than directly observed.
