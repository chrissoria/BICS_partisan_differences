Partisan Differences in Health Behaviors During the COVID-19 Pandemic

Published

March 12, 2026

Abstract

COVID-19 mortality diverged along partisan lines, yet whether individual partisans genuinely differed in health behavior or whether gaps reflect policy environments and geographic sorting remains contested. Existing work documents partisan attitudinal differences but cannot distinguish the independent role of partisan identity from contextual confounds. Using data from the Berkeley Interpersonal Contact Study, a nationally representative survey of 18,233 respondents across six waves (April 2020-May 2021), this study tests competing explanations for partisan gaps in daily social contacts and mask-wearing. Partisan differences persist within shared policy environments, across regions, and after demographic adjustment – a pattern we term “portable polarization.” Objective severity temporarily compresses contact gaps but leaves mask polarization untouched, and stated concern fails to account for behavioral differences, suggesting that expressed attitudes on politicized health topics may not reliably predict protective behavior. Partisan identity operates as an independent driver of health behavior, not reducible to information, policy, or place.

Keywords

COVID-19, political polarization, health behaviors, partisanship, public health

The COVID-19 pandemic revealed a striking pattern in American public health: mortality rates diverged sharply along partisan lines. Counties that voted heavily for Donald Trump in 2020 experienced substantially higher COVID-19 death rates than those that supported Joe Biden (Gollwitzer et al. 2020; Wallace, Goldsmith-Pinkham, and Schwartz 2023). This partisan mortality divide was not a statistical artifact — it emerged during the pandemic and widened over time, particularly after vaccines became available (Wallace et al. 2023). Early in the pandemic, mortality was higher in Democratic-leaning counties, reflecting the initial concentration of cases in urban areas like New York City; but by fall 2020, this pattern reversed, and Republican-leaning counties experienced persistently higher mortality through the remainder of the pandemic (Figure 1).

These aggregate patterns raise the possibility that individual partisans differed in their behavioral responses to the pandemic. But county-level data alone cannot tell us whether such individual-level differences exist, how large they are, or whether they persist once we account for the many other ways that Republican- and Democratic-leaning places differ. Survey research has documented substantial partisan gaps in pandemic-related attitudes — with Republicans expressing less concern about the virus and lower support for mitigation measures (Gadarian, Goodman, and Pepinsky 2021) — and some studies have linked these attitudinal differences to downstream health consequences, finding that political orientation predicted self-rated health and well-being even after accounting for local pandemic severity (Coleman and Andersson 2024). Yet this literature leaves critical questions unanswered: attitudinal studies capture what partisans say but not necessarily what they do, and analyses that link partisanship to health outcomes cannot isolate whether behavioral divergence or geographic sorting drives the association. Before we can explain why partisan identity shapes health behavior, we must first establish whether it does so robustly — independent of policy, place, and demography — and under what conditions partisan identity matters most.

This paper uses data from the Berkeley Interpersonal Contact Study (BICS), a nationally representative survey that collected detailed counts of daily interpersonal contacts and mask usage during the COVID-19 pandemic, to adjudicate between competing explanations for partisan health gaps. Rather than testing a single mechanism, we exploit variation across policy environments, demographic composition, geographic context, perceived severity, and pandemic conditions to ask: which theories survive contact with the empirical patterns? If partisan differences are artifacts of policy or place, they should dissolve within comparable environments; if they reflect demographic sorting, they should close after controlling for covariates; if information and perception fully drive behavior, they should disappear among partisans who assess risk similarly; and if objective severity overrides identity, they should narrow when mortality is highest.

Our findings reveal that partisan differences in health behaviors are remarkably robust. Gaps in social contacts and mask-wearing persist within policy environments, across regions, in both urban and rural areas, and across diverse local partisan compositions — a pattern we term “portable polarization.” At the same time, the magnitude of polarization varies in theoretically revealing ways: objective severity temporarily compresses contact gaps but leaves mask-wearing polarization untouched, and stated concern fails to account for behavioral differences, suggesting that expressed attitudes on politicized health topics may function as markers of group allegiance rather than guides to behavior. These findings advance sociological understanding of how partisan identity — increasingly a master status in American life — shapes health behavior through channels that operate independently of information, policy, and place.

1 Background

1.1 Partisan Differences in Health Behavior

A growing literature documents partisan differences in health-related attitudes and behaviors during the COVID-19 pandemic. In one of the earliest and most influential studies, Gadarian et al. (2021) showed that partisan identity predicted attitudes toward social distancing, mask-wearing, and government intervention even after controlling for demographics, local case rates, and media consumption — suggesting that party identification operated as more than a proxy for other characteristics (see also Allcott et al. 2020; Clinton et al. 2021; Grossman et al. 2020). These attitudinal differences appeared to have real consequences: county-level analyses linked Republican vote share to higher COVID-19 case and death rates, particularly after the initial wave (Gollwitzer et al. 2020; Krieger et al. 2022; Neelon et al. 2021), and Coleman and Andersson (2024) found that political orientation predicted individual self-rated health and well-being during the pandemic even after accounting for local severity. However, most of this work relies on aggregate data or attitudinal measures rather than direct assessments of individual behavior, leaving open the question of whether individual partisans actually behaved differently or whether observed patterns reflect the demographic and geographic sorting of partisans into different environments.

Studies that do examine individual behavior generally confirm partisan differences, but with important limitations. Milosh et al. (2021) found that mask adoption was 12 percentage points lower in Republican-leaning counties even after accounting for local case rates, and Kaushal et al. (2022) documented persistent partisan gaps in social distancing that widened over time (see also Pink et al. 2021). County-level GPS data told a similar story: Allcott et al. (2020) found that Republicans engaged in significantly less social distancing even after controlling for local disease severity (see also Gollwitzer et al. 2020). Clinton et al. (2021), using over one million individual survey responses, found that partisan differences in social activity were initially small but widened substantially over time — with Republicans increasing social activities by 75% compared to 47% for Democrats — and that these gaps far exceeded differences between the most and least severely affected communities. Yet even these studies leave key questions unresolved: the county-level analyses cannot separate individual identity from place-level confounds, and the individual-level survey work, while documenting the temporal arc, did not systematically test whether partisan gaps survive within shared policy, geographic, and demographic contexts. A Democrat in San Francisco and a Republican in rural Alabama differ not only in party identification but in the mandates they face, the norms that surround them, and the information they receive. Existing work has not systematically tested whether partisan behavioral gaps survive within shared contexts.

This study builds on this body of work by moving from aggregate patterns and attitudinal measures to individual-level behavioral data — actual counts of daily contacts and mask usage during specific interactions. Crucially, the design of our data allows us to test whether partisan differences persist within comparable policy, geographic, and demographic contexts — the comparison that existing work has largely been unable to make. By holding context constant and asking whether partisan gaps survive, we can begin to distinguish identity-driven behavioral divergence from the confounding effects of place.

1.2 Partisanship as Social Identity and the Politicization of Health

The transformation of partisanship from a set of policy preferences into a fundamental social identity provides the theoretical foundation for expecting behavioral divergence. Over the past several decades, party identification in the United States has evolved into a primary group attachment that rivals race, religion, and ethnicity in psychological salience (Green et al. 2020; Iyengar and Westwood 2015; Kalmoe and Mason 2022). Americans increasingly select friends, neighbors, and romantic partners based on party affiliation; they express greater social distance from and animosity toward members of the opposing party; and they view events through the lens of partisan group membership rather than ideological principle (Iyengar and Krupenkin 2018; Iyengar and Westwood 2015). Social identities do not merely correlate with behavior — they drive it, through mechanisms including in-group conformity, social pressure, and responsiveness to elite cues (Hoog and Pat-El 2024). Health sociology has increasingly recognized this dynamic: research on health lifestyles shows that health behaviors cluster into integrated patterns undergirded by group-based identities and norms rather than individual calculation (Cockerham 2005; Mollborn, Lawrence, and Onge 2021), suggesting that the same identity-driven processes documented in partisan politics may operate in the health domain.

These dynamics found fertile ground in the domain of public health. Trust in scientific institutions had already been diverging along ideological lines well before COVID-19: Gauchat (2012) documented a long-term decline in trust in science among conservatives beginning in the 1970s — a decline concentrated among the most educated, suggesting that the politicization of science is not a knowledge deficit but an identity-driven process. Parallel data on partisan identification tell a similar story: confidence in science among Republicans dropped sharply beginning in the mid-2000s, even as Democratic confidence held steady or increased (Inc 2021). The pandemic dramatically accelerated this trend (Van Bavel et al. 2024), and recent work in this journal has shown that the resulting polarization is not simply a matter of knowledge deficits: Gauchat (2025) found that conservative distrust of scientific authorities on vaccination was actually magnified among the scientifically literate, suggesting that partisan identity structures how people evaluate expert claims rather than whether they understand them. Questions that epidemiologists viewed as empirical — How severe is the virus? Do masks reduce transmission? Are vaccines safe and effective? — became markers of partisan allegiance (Gadarian et al. 2021; Rothgerber et al. 2020). Republican political leaders and conservative media figures frequently downplayed the pandemic’s severity and questioned the efficacy of protective behaviors, while Democratic leaders emphasized the threat and urged compliance with public health guidance (Gadarian, Goodman, and Pepinsky 2024; Green et al. 2020).

If partisanship functions as a social identity, and if health behaviors became symbols of that identity, we should expect partisans to have diverged in their behavioral responses — not merely because they lived in different policy environments, but because their social identities motivated different responses to the same threat. The question is not whether partisans said different things about the pandemic — they clearly did — but whether they did different things, and whether those behavioral differences can be reduced to the contexts in which partisans are embedded or whether they reflect the independent force of identity itself.

1.3 Competing Explanations for Partisan Health Gaps

We organize our analysis around a set of competing theoretical explanations for partisan behavioral gaps, each of which generates testable predictions about when and where gaps should appear or disappear. This approach — using patterns of contextual variation to adjudicate between theories — allows us to constrain the set of plausible explanations even though we cannot directly test causal mechanisms. The framework yields two core insights: (1) each theory implies that partisan gaps should narrow or vanish under specific conditions, so persistent gaps under those conditions constitute evidence against that theory; and (2) if gaps persist across all conditions where alternative theories predict they should disappear, the surviving explanation is that partisan identity itself drives behavior.

The most straightforward alternative explanations emphasize policy environments and demographic composition. Democrats were concentrated in urban areas governed by officials who implemented stricter lockdowns, mask mandates, and business closures; Republicans lived in areas with fewer restrictions. If partisan gaps are artifacts of these policy differences, they should disappear when we compare partisans within the same policy regime — within strict mandate areas, within lenient areas, and within areas with no mandates. Similarly, if gaps reflect the fact that Democrats and Republicans differ in age, education, urbanicity, and other characteristics associated with health behavior, controlling for these demographics should eliminate the gaps.

A deeper challenge comes from explanations rooted in local context and social conformity. Research has documented powerful contextual effects on health outcomes — for example, Montez et al. (2025) showed that county-level characteristics such as economic conditions, healthcare infrastructure, and social capital predicted mortality rates, and that some of these contextual associations shifted during the pandemic. If behavior is primarily shaped by such local conditions and norms rather than individual identity, partisans in the same environment should behave similarly — a Democrat in a Republican-majority area should be nearly indistinguishable from her Republican neighbors. Baxter-King et al. (2022) provide the most direct test of this prediction for mask-wearing, finding that Republicans were less likely to wear masks as the Republican share of their zip code increased, while Democratic mask-wearing was unaffected by local partisan context — suggesting that contextual effects on visible health behaviors may operate asymmetrically across partisan groups. This “context dominates” prediction can be tested across multiple dimensions: partisan composition, urbanicity, region, and policy environment. If partisan gaps persist across all of these contextual comparisons, it would provide strong evidence that identity drives behavior independent of local culture, what we term “portable polarization”: the idea that partisans carry their behavioral patterns with them regardless of where they live or what surrounds them. Importantly, portable polarization does not require that gaps are identical in every context; it requires only that they persist. Context may shift absolute levels of behavior for all groups without closing the partisan divide, and variation in gap magnitude across contexts is itself analytically informative, potentially revealing which groups are more responsive to local conditions and which are more anchored to nationally circulated partisan cues.

An alternative within-person explanation emphasizes information and risk perception: Republicans and Democrats may have been exposed to different information through divergent media ecosystems and elite messaging (Baum 2011; Graf and Aday 2008), leading to different assessments of pandemic severity that in turn produced different behaviors. Work in this journal supports the plausibility of this pathway: Hawks (2025) showed that perceived legitimacy of public health authorities and shared health values predicted compliance with COVID-19 directives, suggesting that the informational and cultural context through which partisans evaluate health guidance may shape behavioral responses. If this account is correct, partisan gaps should substantially close among partisans who report the same level of concern. However, if stated perceptions are themselves expressions of partisan identity — if partisans report what their group is “supposed” to believe rather than what actually guides their behavior — then perceptions may fail to account for behavioral differences. A related question concerns whether objective pandemic severity can override partisan divergence: if polarization thrives when threats are abstract but narrows when consequences become concrete and personal, gaps should be smaller during high-mortality periods and larger when the threat is distant.

Taken together, these competing explanations define a logical space within which our empirical analysis operates. The question is not whether partisanship correlates with behavior — prior work establishes that it does — but whether that correlation reflects the independent force of partisan identity or can be reduced to policy, place, demographics, information, or objective conditions. The identity account generates a distinctive prediction that separates it from all contextual alternatives: if partisan identity shapes health behavior through the mechanisms described above — group conformity, symbolic boundary maintenance, and responsiveness to nationally circulated elite cues — then its behavioral effects should be portable, traveling with individuals regardless of local context, because the sources of partisan influence (national media, party leaders, co-partisan social networks) are not geographically bounded in the way that policy environments, local norms, and demographic compositions are. If gaps persist after each alternative explanation has been given its best chance to account for them — within shared policy regimes, across regions, in both urban and rural settings, and regardless of local partisan composition — the surviving interpretation is that partisan identity has become a fundamental driver of health behavior, one whose influence operates through channels that transcend place.

1.4 The Current Study

Drawing on the competing-theories framework outlined above, we examine two primary research questions. First, are partisan differences in health behaviors — social contacts, mask-wearing, and vaccine acceptance — robust to policy environment, geographic context, and demographic composition? Second, under what conditions are partisan gaps in health behavior largest versus smallest? We test each competing explanation by examining whether partisan gaps narrow where that theory predicts they should: within shared policy environments, after demographic adjustment, among partisans with similar risk perceptions, during high-severity periods, and across diverse geographic contexts.

We expect the magnitude of partisan gaps to vary across behaviors and conditions. Behaviors that are visible and low-cost — such as mask-wearing — may be more susceptible to partisan signaling than behaviors that are private and high-stakes — such as reducing social contacts during a mortality surge. Similarly, gaps may narrow when the pandemic’s toll is most visible and immediate, as concrete consequences constrain the space for motivated reasoning. These predictions allow us to test not only whether identity matters but how it operates differently across behavioral domains and temporal contexts.

We use data from the Berkeley Interpersonal Contact Study (BICS), a nationally representative survey that collected actual counts of daily interpersonal contacts and detailed mask usage during specific interactions across six waves from April 2020 to May 2021. These behavioral measures — concrete counts rather than abstract self-assessments — provide a more direct window into the behaviors most relevant to disease transmission. Our contribution is primarily descriptive: we do not claim to identify causal mechanisms, but we use the pattern of results across multiple contextual comparisons to constrain the set of plausible explanations for partisan behavioral divergence.

2 Data and Methods

2.1 Data

We use data from the Berkeley Interpersonal Contact Study (BICS), a nationally representative survey that collected detailed information on social contact patterns, protective behaviors, and demographic characteristics during the COVID-19 pandemic in the United States. BICS recruited respondents from an online panel and used weighting methods to generate nationally representative estimates. Respondents were asked how many people they had face-to-face contact with on the day before the survey interview, and detailed information was collected on up to three of these contacts, including each contact’s age, sex, race/ethnicity, mask usage, and physical distance. We analyze waves 1 through 6, spanning April 2020 to May 2021 (N = 18,233). Wave 0 is omitted because it did not record political affiliation in a comparable way. Additional details on survey methodology, questionnaire design, sampling, and weighting are described in Feehan and Mahmud (2021).

The three partisan groups differed on several demographic characteristics (Table 1). Republicans were older on average and more likely to be male. The most striking demographic difference was in racial composition: 85% of Republicans identified as white, compared to 71% of Independents and 60% of Democrats. Democrats were more likely to live in metropolitan areas and to hold a college degree. Our analytic sample overrepresents Democrats (44.9%) relative to national estimates of party identification during the study period — 30% Democrat, 29% Republican, and 39% Independent (Gallup 2021) — while underrepresenting Independents (24.8% vs. 39%). Republicans are represented at roughly their national share (30.3% vs. 29%).

Survey weights are applied throughout to improve representativeness. These weights adjust for age, gender, race/ethnicity, and education, and we additionally rake on party identification to match national targets. Because partisanship is highly correlated with these demographic characteristics, the party-adjusted weights produce estimates nearly identical to the original demographic weights for within-party comparisons (see Section 6.8).

Political party identification was assessed by the panel provider prior to the survey. Upon enrollment in the panel, respondents selected their partisan affiliation from standard categories (Democrat, Republican, Independent, or Prefer not to Say/Other). We exclude respondents in the “Prefer not to Say/Other” category to prevent potential misclassification bias. Because party identification was collected independently of our survey instrument and before the pandemic began for most respondents, it is not subject to priming effects from our health behavior questions. Party identification is among the most stable of political attitudes, typically formed in early adulthood and maintained across the life course (Green 2002), making it unlikely that respondents’ partisan identification changed meaningfully between the panel provider’s measurement and our survey.

Several features of the data warrant consideration. The sample is drawn from an online panel, which may underrepresent populations with limited internet access. Self-reported behavioral measures are subject to social desirability bias and recall error, though the specificity of our measures — actual contact counts for the previous day, mask usage during specific interactions — reduces the scope for such bias relative to abstract attitudinal items. Our assessment of vaccine acceptance is limited to a single wave (May 2021), early in the rollout when eligibility and supply varied across jurisdictions.

2.2 Measures

2.2.1 Dependent Variables

We examine two primary health behaviors measured across all six survey waves, plus vaccination assessed in a single wave.

Social Contacts. Our primary contact measure is the total number of face-to-face contacts respondents reported for the day prior to the survey interview. BICS defined a contact as any in-person interaction involving either physical touch or a conversation of at least a few words. We also examine non-household contacts as a supplementary measure that isolates discretionary social exposure outside the home.

Mask-Wearing. Mask usage is derived from the contact-level alter roster. For each of up to three non-household contacts, respondents indicated whether a face mask was worn during the interaction. We aggregate these reports to construct a respondent-level measure equal to the percentage of non-household contacts during which the respondent wore a mask (0-100 scale). This measure captures actual masking behavior during specific interactions rather than general attitudes toward masks.

Vaccination. Vaccine acceptance, measured in Wave 6 (May 2021) only, is a binary indicator equal to one if the respondent was already vaccinated or stated an intention to vaccinate. Because it was assessed in a single wave, vaccine acceptance is reported descriptively but is not examined in the temporal or contextual analyses; partisan differences are reported in Section 6.1.

2.2.2 Key Independent Variable

Party identification is our primary independent variable. Respondents were classified as Democrats, Republicans, or Independents based on their self-reported affiliation, collected by the panel provider prior to our survey. Democrats serve as the reference category in all regression models.

2.2.3 Control Variables

Local pandemic severity — measured as lagged county-level COVID-19 incidence and mortality rates — is the most theoretically consequential control, as it captures the objective risk environment to which partisans may differentially respond. We link individual survey responses to geographic data at the ZIP code and congressional district levels to characterize local partisan composition (2020 U.S. House election vote share) and policy environment (county-level mask mandate status, classified as strict, less strict, unspecified, or no mandate). Additional demographic controls include age, gender, race/ethnicity, education, income, and urban/rural residence. These variables are included to test whether partisan gaps reflect demographic sorting rather than the independent effect of identity (see Section 6.7 for detailed results).

2.3 Analytical Strategy

The analysis proceeds in three stages. First, we establish partisan differences in health behaviors and test whether they survive demographic controls. Second, we examine whether risk perception and objective pandemic severity can account for the gaps. Third, we test whether gaps persist across geographic, policy, and partisan contexts — the central test of “portable polarization.”

Our empirical strategy is organized around the competing theoretical explanations outlined in the Background. Each explanation predicts that partisan gaps should narrow or disappear under specific conditions; we test each by examining whether gaps persist where that theory predicts they should not. This pattern-based approach allows us to constrain the set of plausible explanations even though we cannot directly identify causal mechanisms with observational data.

To examine whether partisans differentially respond to changes in pandemic severity, we estimate models interacting party identification with lagged local conditions:

\[ Y_{ict} = \beta_1 \text{Party}_i + \beta_2 \log(\text{Incidence}_{c,t-1}) + \beta_3 (\text{Party}_i \times \log(\text{Incidence}_{c,t-1})) + \gamma_c + \mathbf{X}_i'\delta + \epsilon_{ict} \]

where \(Y_{ict}\) is the outcome for individual \(i\) in county \(c\) at time \(t\), \(\text{Party}_i\) indicates partisan affiliation (Democrats as reference), \(\log(\text{Incidence}_{c,t-1})\) is the log-transformed county-level incidence rate during the week prior to the survey, \(\gamma_c\) represents county fixed effects, and \(\mathbf{X}_i\) includes individual-level controls. We estimate parallel models substituting mortality for incidence. Both predictors are standardized (z-score) to facilitate comparison.

For the first research question, we estimate weighted means and regression-adjusted comparisons of health behaviors by party, controlling for demographics. We then stratify by concern level to test the information/perception explanation: if partisan gaps disappear among respondents reporting the same level of concern, the perception account is sufficient; if gaps persist, identity operates through channels beyond information processing.

For the second research question, the county fixed effects specification above tests whether partisan gaps narrow when objective severity rises. By absorbing all time-invariant county characteristics — local political culture, healthcare infrastructure, population density — the model isolates within-county temporal variation, asking: as conditions change within the same county, do partisans adjust their behavior differently? We complement this with regional comparisons using estimated marginal means (emmeans) adjusted for survey wave and local mortality, and with stratification by policy environment and local partisan context.

We assess robustness through weight sensitivity analyses comparing results under demographic-only versus party-raked weights (Section 6.8), and supplementary analyses of partisans living in opposing-party congressional districts (Section 6.7.2).

3 Results

We structure our results as a series of tests designed to adjudicate between the competing theoretical explanations outlined above. We first establish that partisan differences in health behaviors are substantial and survive demographic controls. We then test whether gaps in risk perception can account for behavioral differences, whether objective pandemic severity overrides partisan identity when the stakes are highest, and whether geographic context, policy environments, or local partisan composition can explain the gaps. Each test confronts a specific theory with the empirical patterns; the explanation that survives all tests is the one we retain.

We find substantial partisan differences across all three health behaviors examined (Figure 2). Republicans reported 6.6 total daily contacts compared to 5.3 for Democrats (a gap of 1.2 contacts per day), wore masks during just 54.8% of contacts compared to 65.6% for Democrats (a gap of 11 percentage points), and showed lower vaccine acceptance (34.8% vs. 41.1%; see Section 6.1). These differences persisted after controlling for age, gender, race, education, and urban/rural residence, eliminating the possibility that partisan gaps are artifacts of demographic sorting — the first competing explanation. The partisan gap in mask-wearing was larger than differences observed across other demographic categories including race, gender, and education. Importantly, these gaps are not driven by respondents with minimal out-of-household activity: restricting the sample to those reporting two or more non-household contacts yields comparable partisan differences in both contacts and mask usage (see Section 6.13.1). Because vaccination was assessed only in Wave 6 and cannot be examined longitudinally, the analyses that follow focus on contacts and mask-wearing.

Underlying these behavioral differences were substantial partisan gaps in pandemic risk perception (see Section 6.2): 63% of Democrats reported being “very concerned” about COVID-19 compared to 39% of Republicans — a 23 percentage point gap documented extensively in prior work (Gadarian et al. 2021; Kaushal et al. 2022; Milosh et al. 2021). If these perception differences fully account for the behavioral gaps, then partisans who express the same level of concern should behave similarly. Figure 3 tests this directly by comparing the Republican-Democrat gap within each level of stated concern (see Section 6.2 for levels by party). For social contacts, the partisan gap persists at every level: among the very concerned, Republicans still reported 5.6 daily contacts compared to 5.0 for Democrats, a gap of 0.6 contacts. For mask usage, perceived severity does mitigate polarization: the gap narrows as concern increases, and among the “very concerned,” Republicans and Democrats report nearly identical mask usage (72% vs. 72%).

Interaction models formally confirm these patterns (see Section 6.4). For contacts, the Republican x Very Concerned interaction is statistically significant and negative (\(\beta\) = -1.01, p = 0.03), indicating that concern modestly narrows the partisan contact gap — but does not close it. For masks, the interaction is large and significant (\(\beta\) = 12.2, p < 0.001). The mechanism is clear: among the very concerned, Republicans wore masks during 72% of contacts, nearly identical to Democrats (72%). But this convergence reflects Republicans responding more sharply to concern: Republicans increased mask usage by 43 percentage points from “not at all concerned” to “very concerned,” compared to 36 points for Democrats. In sum, perceived severity mitigates partisan polarization for both behaviors, though the effect is substantially larger for mask usage than for social contacts. These results weigh against the information and perception explanation as a complete account: if partisan behavioral differences were simply downstream of different risk perceptions, they should disappear among partisans who express the same level of concern. They do not — particularly for contacts, where the gap persists at every concern level. Perception matters, but it is insufficient on its own to explain partisan behavioral divergence.

These aggregate gaps were not constant over time. Figure 4 tracks partisan gaps across survey waves alongside local mortality rates. The contact gap was smallest during high-mortality periods — early in the pandemic and during the winter 2020-21 surge (gaps of 0.3-0.7 contacts) — and largest when mortality was low (3.5 contacts in September 2020). Regression models confirm that Republicans were more responsive to mortality than Democrats, reducing contacts more sharply when deaths rose (see Section 6.11 for detailed coefficients). The mask gap, by contrast, grew steadily from 6 to 15 percentage points regardless of mortality, showing no convergence during the winter surge.

This divergence between contacts and masks offers a partial verdict on the “objective severity overrides identity” explanation. Objective severity can temporarily compress partisan gaps for contacts — a private, high-stakes behavior where the personal consequences of not reducing exposure are immediate and concrete — but it does not override identity for mask-wearing, a visible, low-cost behavior that had become an entrenched marker of partisan identity. The most telling evidence comes from the winter 2020-21 surge: Republicans reduced their contacts substantially, converging toward Democratic levels, yet simultaneously reported their lowest levels of concern across all waves — attitudes polarized while behavior converged. This disconnect suggests that expressed attitudes had become performative markers of group allegiance rather than genuine guides to behavior (see Section 6.2.1 for detailed analysis). Neither perception nor severity can fully account for the partisan behavioral divide.

The final set of tests concerns geographic context. If partisan gaps reflect regional culture or adaptation to local norms, they should narrow or disappear within shared geographic settings. Figure 5 displays Republican-Democrat gaps across census regions, adjusted for survey wave and local mortality. Partisan gaps persist in every region, though with notable variation: the contact gap ranged from 0.9 contacts in the South to 1.7 in the West; the mask gap from 8.3 percentage points in the South to 14.6 in the West. Importantly, where gaps are smaller, as in the South, this reflects Democrats behaving less cautiously (5.5 contacts vs. 4.9 elsewhere), not Republicans becoming more cautious (see Section 6.12 for detailed breakdowns and Section 6.14 for comprehensive behavior levels by party across demographic and contextual subgroups). Context shifts absolute levels of behavior but does not close the partisan gap.

These geographic patterns are robust to alternative contextual explanations. Partisan gaps persisted within each mask mandate regime — strict, less strict, and no mandate — eliminating the “it’s just policy” explanation (see Section 6.12.2). Gaps remained stable after adjusting for urbanicity and demographics (see Section 6.7). Supplementary analyses of partisans living in opposing-party congressional districts tell a consistent story: while partisans adjust modestly toward local norms, the gap persists in both same-party and cross-party contexts (Section 6.7.2). These findings eliminate the remaining competing explanations: partisan gaps do not disappear among people living under the same mandates, in the same regions, or among members of the opposing party.

Taken together, we can assess the scorecard of competing explanations. Demographic composition does not account for partisan gaps: they persist after controlling for age, gender, race, education, and urbanicity. Information and risk perception offer a partial account for mask-wearing but fail for contacts, where gaps remain at every level of stated concern. Objective severity temporarily compresses contact gaps during mortality surges but leaves mask polarization untouched — and the disconnect between stated concern and behavior during the winter surge undermines the premise that perceptions faithfully guide action. Policy environments do not explain the gaps: they persist within strict, lenient, and no-mandate areas alike. Regional culture shifts absolute levels of behavior but does not close the partisan divide, and even partisans living among members of the opposing party maintain behavioral differences.

The surviving explanation is that partisan identity itself drives behavioral polarization — what we term “portable polarization.” Partisans carry their behavioral patterns with them across diverse environments, even as they adjust modestly toward local norms. Each competing factor matters at the margins — perception narrows the mask gap among the very concerned, mortality temporarily compresses the contact gap, and regional context shifts absolute levels — but none eliminates the fundamental partisan divide. The gaps are not artifacts of Republicans and Democrats sorting into different environments; they are features of partisan identity itself.

4 Discussion

A long tradition in sociology treats social identities as structuring forces that shape behavior through mechanisms of group conformity, boundary maintenance, and durable dispositions (Bourdieu 1984; Lamont 2000; Tajfel and Turner 1979). In the health domain specifically, Cockerham (2005) argued that health behaviors are not reducible to individual agency but are structured by collective, group-level forces — an insight that health lifestyles research has since confirmed across multiple populations and behavioral domains (Mollborn et al. 2021). Our findings extend this tradition by identifying partisan identity as a newly potent structuring force in health behavior. Partisan differences in social contacts, mask-wearing, and vaccine acceptance during the COVID-19 pandemic were not only substantial — often exceeding differences by race, gender, or education — but proved remarkably resistant to every alternative explanation we tested, consistent with Clinton et al. (2021)’s finding that partisanship was roughly 27 times more important than public health concerns in predicting pandemic mobility behavior. They persisted within shared policy environments, across geographic regions, regardless of local partisan composition, and after controlling for demographics. This pattern of “portable polarization” — behavioral differences that prove remarkably robust across diverse contexts, even as partisans adjust modestly toward local norms — is consistent with the claim that partisanship has become a fundamental social identity in American life, one that shapes health behavior directly rather than merely correlating with the environments in which partisans are embedded.

The specific patterns of variation in partisan gaps offer insight into the mechanisms through which identity operates. Social contacts — a private, high-stakes behavior — provide a particularly informative theoretical test because staying home, avoiding gatherings, and reducing daily interactions are largely unobservable by one’s political community; they cannot easily function as signals of partisan allegiance in the way that visible behaviors can. Contact gaps narrowed during high-mortality periods, suggesting that concrete, undeniable consequences can temporarily override partisan divergence when the behavior is private and the stakes are immediate. Mask-wearing — a visible, low-cost behavior — showed steadily widening polarization regardless of severity, consistent with its emergence as what Goffman (1963) would recognize as a stigma symbol: a publicly legible marker that instantly communicates group membership, making it a site of impression management rather than a calculated response to risk. Research on social norms and COVID-19 compliance has distinguished public-facing from private protective behaviors, finding that mandatory mask policies shaped not only compliance but social perceptions of others’ behavior (Betsch et al. 2020), while work on identity-protective cognition has shown that culturally diverse individuals selectively credit and dismiss evidence in patterns that reflect their group’s beliefs — a dynamic that intensifies rather than diminishes among the most scientifically literate (Kahan 2017). Baxter-King et al. (2022) gesture toward this visibility mechanism, noting that mask-wearing is “publicly observable” compared to other mitigation strategies and finding that local partisan context shaped Republican but not Democratic mask-wearing. Our findings extend this framework by systematically comparing behaviors that differ in social visibility within the same sample — a test that prior work, including Baxter-King et al., was not designed to make. That masking diverged more sharply by partisanship than contact reduction did demonstrates that the symbolic dimension of health behavior was politically activated in a way that private protective behaviors were not. The logic of this comparison is diagnostic: if partisan gaps were driven entirely by generalized health lifestyle dispositions or divergent risk perceptions, both behaviors should show similar partisan divergence, since both reduce disease transmission. An alternative reading might attribute the divergence to differences in personal cost rather than social visibility, since reducing contacts entails greater material sacrifice than wearing a mask. But this account generates the wrong temporal prediction: if behavioral gaps primarily reflect cost sensitivity, mask gaps should also narrow during mortality surges, since the health cost of not masking rises alongside mortality. Instead, only the private, high-cost behavior responded to heightened risk while the visible, low-cost behavior did not, a pattern more consistent with a social observability mechanism in which the identity costs of deviating from one’s group norm on a publicly legible behavior offset the health benefits of compliance. Had masking and contact patterns tracked each other closely within partisan groups, that would suggest a coherent underlying behavioral disposition, the kind of integrated health lifestyle that Cockerham (2005) describes. That they diverged, specifically in the direction predicted by social visibility, points instead to identity-driven processes operating selectively on publicly observable behaviors. We note, however, that the widening mask gap is consistent with more than one identity-based mechanism. The pattern could reflect active signaling, in which partisans consciously display or withhold masks to communicate group membership, but it could equally reflect the gradual crystallization of group norms: Democrats habituating to mask-wearing as an unremarkable default within their social networks while Republicans consolidated an anti-mask norm within theirs. These mechanisms are not mutually exclusive, and both are fundamentally identity-driven, but they differ in the degree of intentionality involved. Our data cannot distinguish between them, though the steady, monotonic widening of the gap over time is perhaps more consistent with norm crystallization than with episodic signaling in response to specific encounters. What our data do establish is that whatever the specific mechanism, it operated selectively on the visible behavior and not on the private one, confirming that social observability is a necessary condition for the identity process at work.

The geographic distribution of partisan gaps further illuminates how context and identity interact. Where gaps were smaller, as in the South, the narrowing reflected Democrats behaving less cautiously rather than Republicans becoming more cautious, a pattern visible across demographic and contextual subgroups (Section 6.14): context shifted absolute levels of behavior without closing the partisan divide. This asymmetric pattern is consistent with Baxter-King et al. (2022)‘s finding that local partisan context shaped Republican but not Democratic mask-wearing, and suggests more broadly that the two parties’ health behaviors may be anchored to different reference points. If Republican behavior is primarily shaped by nationally circulated partisan cues and media ecosystems that do not vary much by region, while Democratic behavior responds more to local norms and conditions, we would expect exactly the pattern we observe: gaps that persist in every region but vary in magnitude as local conditions pull one group toward the behavioral baseline of its surroundings. Regional variation in gap size therefore does not undermine the portable polarization thesis; rather, it specifies the channel through which context operates, shifting the behavior of one group more than the other while leaving the fundamental partisan divide intact.

Earlier work using GPS mobility data found that partisan identity moderated responses to elite messaging from the pandemic’s earliest days: Democratic-leaning counties responded about twice as strongly to governors’ stay-at-home recommendations as Republican-leaning counties, even after accounting for demographics, occupational structure, and local case rates (Grossman et al. 2020). Our individual-level data are consistent with this early divergence but reveal an additional dynamic: the behavioral gaps we measure widened over time, particularly for visible behaviors like mask-wearing, suggesting that what began as differential responsiveness to elite cues crystallized into durable identity markers as the pandemic progressed. The temporal pattern for mask-wearing mirrors what Clinton et al. (2021) documented for stated concern: in their data, Democrats maintained consistently high concern while Republicans became increasingly less concerned over time, with the gap accelerating after partisan elite cues intensified. Our mask-wearing data follow this same widening trajectory — but our contact data do not, revealing a critical disconnect between stated concern and actual behavior. During the winter 2020-21 surge, Republicans reported their lowest concern levels yet reduced contacts substantially, suggesting that expressed attitudes had become performative signals of group allegiance while behavior tracked objective conditions. This finding challenges the assumption, common in survey-based health research, that stated attitudes reliably predict or explain protective behavior on politicized health topics. More fundamentally, the persistence of partisan behavioral gaps even after accounting for perceived severity suggests that something beyond risk cognition is driving behavior — the behavior has decoupled from the belief. This residual gap is precisely where identity-based theories do their explanatory work: social categorization processes that make partisan membership salient in everyday encounters (Tajfel and Turner 1979), impression management pressures that make visible behaviors like mask-wearing sites of stigma or prestige within one’s community (Goffman 1963), and symbolic boundary maintenance that transforms health practices into markers of group belonging (Lamont 2000). The practical implication is that public health messaging designed to increase reported concern may change what people say without changing what they do — and that interventions targeting the objective risk environment (e.g., making consequences visible and local) may be more effective than those targeting stated attitudes. Experimental evidence underscores the identity dynamics at work: (Pink et al. 2021) found that Republican elite endorsements increased vaccination intentions among Republicans, while Democratic endorsements produced backlash, actually reducing Republicans’ willingness to encourage others to vaccinate. This suggests that effective public health communication on politicized topics may need to work through partisan identity rather than against it — though such an approach risks further entrenching the very polarization it seeks to navigate.

This study contributes to the sociology of health by demonstrating that partisan identity operates as an independent driver of health behavior, not reducible to policy, information, or place — extending recent work in this journal showing that partisan polarization structures trust in scientific authorities in ways that intensify among the most knowledgeable (Gauchat 2025) from the domain of attitudes to the domain of behavior. The competing-theories framework offers a generalizable approach for descriptive studies seeking to constrain causal explanations: by systematically testing where each theory predicts gaps should narrow, we identify the explanation that survives the most stringent comparisons. Several limitations warrant consideration alongside these contributions. Our data are observational and cross-sectional within each wave, precluding strong causal claims; we observe associations between partisan identity and health behavior but cannot definitively rule out unmeasured confounders. All behavioral measures are self-reported, raising several interrelated concerns about partisan survey bias. Democrats may under-report contacts and over-report mask usage to conform to perceived group expectations, just as Republicans may do the reverse; Yair and Huber (2020) have shown that standard survey items can confound sincere partisan differences with expressive responding — insincere answers that communicate partisan allegiance rather than actual perceptions or behaviors. If such dynamics operate in our data, we would be capturing identity signaling in the survey response itself rather than in the underlying behavior — though notably, this would constitute the same identity-driven mechanism we theorize about, operating at the level of survey response rather than health behavior. Additionally, Clinton, Lapinski, and Trussler (2022) documented differential partisan nonresponse in surveys conducted during this period, with Republicans — particularly those least engaged with public health guidance — being less likely to participate. If this selection operates in our data, it would likely attenuate the partisan gaps we estimate, because the Republicans missing from our sample are plausibly those with the most contacts and least mask usage; our estimates would thus represent a conservative lower bound on the true behavioral divergence (see Section 6.8 for further discussion). The specificity of our measures — actual contact counts for the previous day, mask usage during specific recalled interactions — reduces the scope for expressive responding relative to abstract attitudinal items, but cannot eliminate it. Crucially, however, even if expressive responding inflates the mask-wearing gap, the contact measure provides a partial check: counting how many people you spoke with face-to-face yesterday is a concrete, verifiable recall task with less room for partisan motivated reasoning than reporting whether you “usually” wear a mask, and partisan gaps in contacts persist even during high-mortality periods when expressive incentives to minimize concern were strongest among Republicans. Our survey waves span April 2020 to May 2021, capturing a critical period but not the later Delta and Omicron waves or the longer arc of vaccine polarization; Wallace et al. (2023) documented that the partisan mortality gap widened substantially after vaccines became available, suggesting that the behavioral divergence we observe likely intensified beyond our observation window. These temporal boundaries are also a strength: by examining a period when partisan scripts were actively forming, we observe polarization as it developed rather than after it crystallized.

Each limitation suggests a direction for future work. Longitudinal designs following the same individuals over time could establish whether partisan identity causes behavioral change or whether partisan self-sorting into different behavioral patterns precedes identity consolidation. Comparative studies across health crises — including non-pandemic contexts such as gun violence, environmental health, or reproductive health — could test whether portable polarization is specific to COVID-19 or characterizes partisan identity’s influence on health behavior more broadly; evidence that ideology shaped vaccine hesitancy even before the pandemic (Baumgaertner, Carlisle, and Justwan 2018) suggests that the dynamics we document may have deeper roots. Experimental work could examine whether making mortality consequences salient narrows partisan gaps in real time, providing a causal test of the severity mechanism we observe descriptively. Finally, network-based studies could illuminate the social pathways through which partisan behavioral norms are transmitted and reinforced — Mosleh et al. (2021) found that shared partisanship dramatically increases social tie formation, suggesting that partisan homophily may create echo chambers for health behavior norms that amplify the divergence we document.

Our findings suggest that the familiar sociological insight — that social identities structure behavior through mechanisms beyond individual preference — applies forcefully to partisan identity in the health domain. The implication is not simply that partisans disagreed about COVID-19, but that the same dynamics of group conformity, symbolic boundary-making, and habitus that sociologists have long studied in the contexts of race, religion, and class now operate through partisanship as well. Understanding how to bridge these divides during health crises — or, more ambitiously, how to prevent health from becoming partisan terrain in the first place — requires not just better information or clearer messaging, but a sociological understanding of why people adopt the health behaviors their group expects of them, even when those behaviors may not serve their material interests.

The main text documents substantial partisan differences in pandemic risk perception, with Democrats more likely than Republicans to report being “very concerned” about COVID-19. Figure 7 displays the percentage of each partisan group reporting being “very concerned” about COVID-19 across survey waves. Figure 8 shows the Democrat-Republican gap in this measure over time, with weekly COVID-19 cases shown in the background for context.

The perception gap was smallest at the beginning of the pandemic: just 11.3 percentage points in Apr ’20. By Dec ’20, the gap had grown to 28.8 percentage points. All three partisan groups showed declining concern over time, particularly from the winter surge through spring 2021 as vaccines became widely available. The winter 2020–21 surge presents a more complex picture. Mortality rose sharply, and Republicans did reduce their contacts in response—yet their stated concern continued to decline. This disconnect between mortality-driven behavior and diverging perceptions suggests that by this point, expressed concern had become entangled with partisan identity in ways that were resistant to objective conditions. The early-pandemic alignment, when partisanship had not yet fully politicized COVID-19, may represent a window before symbolic boundaries hardened. Once the pandemic became a site of partisan conflict, stated perceptions became partly performative—reflecting group allegiance rather than risk assessment—even as underlying behavior continued to respond to concrete threat.

6.4 APPENDIX D. Interaction Models: Concern and Mortality as Moderators

To formally test whether stated concern and objective mortality rates moderate partisan gaps in health behaviors, we estimate interaction models of the form:

\[\text{Behavior} = \beta_0 + \beta_1(\text{Republican}) + \beta_2(\text{Concern}) + \beta_3(\text{Mortality}) + \beta_4(\text{Rep} \times \text{Concern}) + \beta_5(\text{Rep} \times \text{Mortality}) + \text{FE}\]

In this specification, the partisan gap equals \(\beta_1 + \beta_4(\text{Concern}) + \beta_5(\text{Mortality})\). A negative \(\beta_4\) indicates that higher concern narrows the partisan gap; a negative \(\beta_5\) indicates that higher mortality narrows it. Concern is measured as a binary indicator for reporting being “very concerned” about COVID-19; mortality is the standardized log of the previous week’s county-level mortality rate.

We estimate two versions of this model to distinguish between national/temporal patterns and local variation:

  1. Wave FE model: Includes wave fixed effects and controls for metropolitan status. This captures how the partisan gap varies with national pandemic severity (high-mortality waves vs. low-mortality waves).

  2. County FE model: Includes county fixed effects. This asks: within the same county, when local mortality rises, does the partisan gap narrow? This isolates the effect of local conditions from all time-invariant county characteristics.

Figure 16 displays the results from both specifications.

Wave FE Model (National/Temporal Patterns): For contacts, the baseline Republican-Democrat gap is 1.45 contacts per day. The concern interaction is statistically significant and negative (\(\beta\) = -1.01, p = 0.03), indicating that stated concern does modestly narrow the partisan contact gap. The mortality interaction is negative (\(\beta\) = 0.11, p = 0.573): during high-mortality waves, the partisan gap narrows.

For masks, the baseline gap is 10.4 percentage points. The concern interaction is large and significant (\(\beta\) = 12.2, p < 0.001): being “very concerned” substantially narrows the mask gap. The mortality interaction is smaller and in the opposite direction (\(\beta\) = 1.7).

County FE Model (Local Variation): The county FE model asks whether the partisan gap narrows within counties when local mortality rises. For contacts, the mortality interaction is -0.28 (p = 0.424), indicating that local mortality conditions also moderate the partisan gap. For masks, the local mortality interaction is 4.7 (p = 0.001).

Both specifications support the same conclusion: concern moderates the mask gap but not the contact gap; mortality moderates the contact gap but not the mask gap. This pattern holds whether we examine national trends (wave FE) or local variation (county FE), suggesting it reflects a genuine behavioral response to pandemic conditions rather than a compositional artifact.

6.6 APPENDIX F. Partisan Differences Within Policy Environments

A potential alternative explanation for partisan differences in health behaviors is that Democrats disproportionately reside in jurisdictions with stricter COVID-19 policies, and their behavior simply reflects compliance with local regulations rather than partisan preferences. To address this concern, we examine whether partisan differences persist within the same policy environment.

Figure 19 and Figure 20 display mean contact rates and mask usage by party, stratified by county-level mask mandate policy (No Mandate, Less Strict, or Strict). If policy environment fully explained partisan differences, we would expect the gaps between parties to disappear within each policy category.

The results clearly demonstrate that partisan differences are not simply artifacts of differential policy exposure. Within each policy environment, Republicans reported more daily contacts than Democrats: the gap was 1.4 contacts in No Mandate areas, 1.0 contacts in Less Strict areas, and 1.3 contacts in Strict mandate areas. Similarly, Democrats reported higher mask usage than Republicans across all policy contexts: the gap was 9 percentage points in No Mandate areas, 11 percentage points in Less Strict areas, and 10 percentage points in Strict mandate areas.

These findings suggest that partisan identity shapes health behaviors above and beyond the effects of local policy. While stricter policies are associated with higher mask usage overall, the Republican-Democrat gap in mask-wearing remains remarkably stable across policy environments. This pattern is consistent with partisanship operating through mechanisms other than policy compliance—such as differential risk perception, social norms within partisan networks, or ideological commitments—that persist regardless of the local regulatory context.

6.8 APPENDIX H. Weight Sensitivity Analysis

To assess the robustness of our findings to different weighting approaches, we compare results across three scenarios: (1) unweighted estimates, (2) original demographic weights that adjust for age, gender, race/ethnicity, and education, and (3) raked weights that additionally adjust for party identification to match national targets from Gallup polling (30% Democrat, 29% Republican, 39% Independent; Gallup (2021)). The raking procedure adjusts for potential correlations between party identification and demographic characteristics in our sample.

A key finding from this analysis is that raked weights and original demographic weights produce identical within-party estimates for all outcomes. This equivalence occurs because the raking procedure applies a constant multiplier to all observations within each party group. When calculating within-party means or party-specific regression coefficients, this constant multiplier cancels out. Raking only matters for population-level estimates that pool across partisan groups, where it adjusts the relative contribution of each party to the overall estimate.

We observe small differences between unweighted and weighted estimates. The Republican-Democrat contact gap is 1.33 contacts per day using unweighted data compared to 1.25 contacts per day with demographic weights—a 6% reduction. The vaccine acceptance gap is -18.6 percentage points (unweighted) versus -17.0 percentage points (weighted)—a 9% reduction in the absolute gap. Mask usage differences remain stable across all weighting scenarios, with the Republican-Democrat gap at approximately -10.8 percentage points regardless of weights used.

Importantly, all partisan differences remain highly significant (p < 0.001) across all three weighting scenarios. These results demonstrate that our findings are robust to the choice of weighting specification and that the substantive conclusions about partisan gaps in health behaviors do not depend on how we adjust for sample composition.

6.8.1 Direction of Potential Selection Bias

Beyond weighting, a distinct concern is that participation in an online panel survey may itself be correlated with partisanship in ways that bias our estimates. Clinton et al. (2022) documented that Republicans were less likely than Democrats to participate in surveys conducted during the pandemic, and that this differential nonresponse was concentrated among the least politically engaged and least health-conscious Republicans — precisely those most likely to have maintained high contact rates and low mask usage. If this pattern extends to the BICS sample, the Republicans in our data would be more compliant and health-conscious than the Republican population as a whole, meaning that the partisan gaps we estimate are likely attenuated relative to the true population differences.

The logic runs as follows. Willingness to participate in a university-administered health survey plausibly selects for civic engagement, health consciousness, and openness to public health messaging. Among Democrats, these traits are broadly aligned with partisan norms, so selection likely has modest effects on the composition of Democratic respondents. Among Republicans, however, these traits are in tension with partisan signals that framed pandemic precautions as overreach; the Republicans who opted into the survey are therefore plausibly those most willing to deviate from the strongest partisan behavioral scripts. The most disengaged Republicans — those who dismissed COVID-19 precautions most thoroughly — are the least likely to appear in our sample. This asymmetric selection would compress the observed partisan gap from the Republican side, making our estimates conservative.

This reasoning is consistent with the weight sensitivity results above: adjusting for observable demographic imbalances produces only modest changes in partisan gaps, suggesting that the respondents who did participate are reasonably representative on observable characteristics. But to the extent that unobservable characteristics (e.g., distrust of institutions, disengagement from health monitoring) are correlated with both nonparticipation and extreme behavior, the true gaps are likely larger than what we report.

6.13 APPENDIX M. Non-Household Contacts and Distributional Robustness

A potential concern with the total-contacts measure is that partisan differences could reflect household composition rather than discretionary social behavior—for example, if Republicans live in larger households. A related concern is that the mean contact count could be pulled by outliers in the right tail, inflating the apparent partisan gap. This appendix addresses both issues by (1) decomposing contacts into household and non-household components and (2) estimating the partisan gap using negative binomial models and quantile regressions that are robust to distributional assumptions.

Figure 42 shows the decomposition of mean daily contacts into household and non-household components by party, pooled across all survey waves. The Republican–Democrat gap in total contacts (1.29 contacts/day) is overwhelmingly driven by non-household contacts (1.21 contacts/day, or 94% of the total gap), with household contacts contributing only 0.07 contacts/day. This indicates that the partisan contact gap reflects differences in discretionary social behavior—not household composition.

Figure 43 shows non-household contacts by party across survey waves. The partisan gap in discretionary contacts is present at every wave, generally widening during fall 2020 (wave 3) before narrowing again.

Finally, Figure 44 presents the estimated partisan gap from multiple model specifications, demonstrating that the Republican–Democrat difference is robust to distributional assumptions. The negative binomial models account for overdispersion common in count data, while quantile regressions show that the gap is not driven by extreme values in the right tail—it is present at the 25th, 50th, and 75th percentiles of the contact distribution.

Across all specifications, Democrats report significantly fewer contacts than Republicans. The negative binomial models (Panel A) show that Democrats have a lower rate of both total and non-household contacts relative to Republicans, confirming that the gap is not an artifact of the OLS functional form. The quantile regressions (Panel B) reveal that the Democrat–Republican gap is present not only at the median but also at the 25th and 75th percentiles of the contact distribution, ruling out the possibility that the mean difference is driven by a small number of high-contact Republican outliers. The OLS estimate for non-household contacts corroborates the negative binomial finding. Together, these results demonstrate that the partisan contact gap is (a) concentrated in discretionary non-household contacts, (b) robust to modeling overdispersed count data, and (c) present across the full distribution of contacts—not merely an artifact of the mean.
